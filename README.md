# Blueprint for Post-Scarcity: A 75-Year Strategic Techno-Economic Roadmap

## I. Strategic Imperative and Foundational Principles
### 1.1. Defining Post-Scarcity and the Space-Enabled Model
The pursuit of a post-scarcity society (PS) does not hinge on the existence of speculative technologies, such as matter-energy replicators, which are excluded from this blueprint. Instead, PS is functionally defined by the widespread availability of basic human needs—food, shelter, energy, healthcare, and education—to all citizens, thereby decoupling survival from traditional labor constraints. This state of abundance must be grounded in two pillars: scalable automation and the eventual access to near-infinite material wealth sourced from extraterrestrial bodies, including rare metals like platinum-group metals (PGMs), water, volatiles, and potentially Helium-3.

### 1.2. The Scarcity Trap: Analyzing the Interconnected Failure of Linear Economics
The primary obstacle to initiating a sustainable transition is the reliance on traditional economic metrics that fundamentally misrepresent societal welfare. The prevailing measure, Gross Domestic Product (GDP), fails to differentiate between productive economic activity and compensatory activity, treating environmental cleanup, crime response, and resource depletion as positive contributions to growth. This methodology incentivizes resource consumption and environmental destruction, thereby ensuring perpetual scarcity.
A society striving for true, sustainable abundance must adopt a metric that accounts for these external costs. The transition mandates the adoption of the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI). GPI is distinct from GDP because it subtracts the financial costs associated with environmental degradation, resource depletion, and social harm (such as crime and pollution) from the economic growth measured by GDP. The shift to this metric is not purely theoretical; several US state governments have already formally adopted GPI studies. Scaling this approach nationally and internationally is critical to ensure that economic investments made during Phase 1—particularly in clean energy and environmental preservation—are accurately valued. Without this immediate policy alignment, the long-term goal of abundance risks being undermined by an inaccurate accounting system that perpetually conflates cost with progress.

### 1.3. Overview of the Three-Phase Transition Framework (2025–2075+)
The roadmap is structured in three consecutive phases designed to systematically dismantle scarcity constraints. The initial phase, Phase 1: Short-Term Foundations (2025–2045), focuses on Terrestrial Decoupling, establishing economic, energy, and circular foundations on Earth. Phase 2: Medium-Term Scaling (2045–2075) involves globalizing these technological and policy advancements, preparing the necessary orbital infrastructure, and achieving economic stabilization. Phase 3: Long-Term Realization (2075+) integrates space-enabled abundance, utilizing extraterrestrial resources to offload industrial activity and restore the terrestrial environment.

## II. Phase 1: Short-Term Foundations (2025–2045) – Terrestrial Decoupling
### 2.1. Economic Restructuring: Funding Abundance through Collective Provisioning (UBS)
To address immediate human needs while prioritizing sustainable resource use, the blueprint advocates for implementing Universal Basic Services (UBS) over Universal Basic Income (UBI). UBS, which provides direct access to essentials such as housing, healthcare, and education via collective provisioning systems, addresses the production side of the economy, minimizing the resource waste associated with individual market purchasing and optimizing resource efficiency.
Proposed Funding Mechanism Analysis: Progressive Taxation of Automation Profits
Widespread automation, while crucial for productivity, inherently reduces the labor share of revenue, shifting wealth disproportionately toward capital owners. This displacement threatens social stability and erodes the traditional labor-based tax system. A progressive tax on automation profits or capital utilization is strategically necessary to recapture the value generated by machine substitution.
This policy mechanism functions as a dynamic stabilizer for the transition. By linking the funding of the core social solution (UBS) directly to the economic damage caused by automation (job displacement), the policy transforms automation from a societal threat into the mandated engine of the post-scarcity transition. To avoid discouraging innovation, the tax model should focus on taxing assets and capital ratios, rather than increasing existing corporate tax rates or taxing the machines themselves. The feasibility of integrating essential services via public infrastructure is exemplified in various global and foundational economy frameworks.

### 2.2. Industrial Ecology: Mandating the Circular Transition
Phase 1 requires a radical transition to a circular economy, mandating that products are designed for durability, reuse, and recycling, with targets of 80–90% waste reduction. This goal is substantiated by existing technology: the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) projects that a combination of existing policies and technologies could reduce plastic pollution by 80% by 2040.
Regulatory frameworks must include mandatory Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) laws, which legally hold manufacturers accountable for the end-of-life management and recovery of their products. This ensures value retention across the supply chain. Enforcing this radical circularity in the short term is a non-negotiable prerequisite for long-term abundance. If the terrestrial economy remains structurally linear, the eventual introduction of vast quantities of extraterrestrial materials (such as PGMs from asteroids) in Phase 3 would merely feed a larger waste stream, accelerating consumption and rendering the space resource model unsustainable. The mandatory 80%+ circularity mandate must be achieved by 2045 to guarantee that space wealth supplements an efficient, closed-loop system, thereby preventing the re-scarcitization of resources.

### 2.3. Achieving Energy and Food Sovereignty
Accelerated Renewable Adoption and Fusion Energy
The foundation of post-scarcity lies in achieving near-infinite, clean energy. While scaled solar and wind are critical, the elimination of energy scarcity requires a successful transition to fusion power. Significant public and private investments, exceeding $6 billion in private equity, indicate the rapidly increasing Technical Readiness Level (TRL) for commercial fusion. Major advances in areas like high-temperature superconductors and Artificial Intelligence are accelerating this R&D. The US Department of Energy (DOE) Milestone program is actively partnering with private companies to design commercial pilot fusion power plants, targeting feasibility within Phase 1 or early Phase 2.
Precision Agriculture and Controlled Environment Agriculture (CEA)
Food sovereignty is achieved by decoupling production from vulnerable arable land. Vertical farming offers substantial gains, providing 10 to 20 times more crops per square foot annually and requiring 70–90% less water than traditional farming. However, these CEA systems demand intensive energy input for lighting and climate control. Similarly, emerging biotechnologies, such as cultured meat production, currently require pharmaceutical-grade purification of growth media, which is energy-intensive and can lead to a greater environmental impact than traditional farming until scaled efficiencies are realized.
This situation reveals a critical systemic dependency: the goal of food sovereignty through CEA and advanced biotech is entirely contingent upon the successful, scalable commercialization of cheap, clean fusion power. Without energy abundance, these production methods simply shift scarcity from land and water to carbon emissions and power costs. Therefore, accelerated funding and protection of fusion R&D must be prioritized above all other single technological pursuits in Phase 1, as its success validates the entire sustainable food component of UBS.

## III. Policy De-Scarcitization and Restriction Removal
### 3.1. Removing Terrestrial Policy Impediments (US/Global Focus)
Several restrictive policies must be reformed to accelerate the PS transition. First, the elimination of US fossil fuel subsidies, which are estimated to range between $10 billion and $52 billion annually , is essential. Targeting the elimination of approximately $20 billion of these subsidies provides immediate capital for funding early UBS initiatives and green technology R&D.
Streamlining NEPA Review and Reforming Green IP
Permitting delays under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) have substantially slowed critical renewable infrastructure projects, with wind projects often requiring up to 45 months. Recent reforms, such as those implemented by the DOE under the BUILDER Act, aim to reduce these delays through flexible guidance. To further de-risk projects, policy adjustments must include shortening the statute of limitations for judicial challenges to 60–120 days, thereby accelerating the deployment of renewable energy capacity.
The intellectual property (IP) framework requires strategic reform. Traditional 20-year patent durations incentivize private R&D but impede the rapid, global diffusion necessary for urgent climate solutions. Studies suggest that while longer patents may sometimes mitigate pollution in certain contexts, they can exacerbate inefficiencies if environmental regulators are slow to update standards. To resolve this paradox, the state must significantly increase public funding for high-risk green R&D. In return, the resulting technologies must be subjected to accelerated examination and compulsory, compensated licensing. This model shifts the incentive from monopolistic market control to guaranteed governmental procurement and rapid global deployment, prioritizing the collective benefit of innovation.
Reforming Antitrust for Cooperative Automation
Traditional antitrust enforcement focuses narrowly on consumer price harm. However, cooperative automation, such as allowing technology monopolies to share AI infrastructure for public goods like UBS logistics and resource optimization, requires a different approach. Reform must allow for controlled and monitored cooperation among major platforms and AI developers specifically for public benefit, while simultaneously ensuring ongoing regulation against profit-hoarding monopolies.

### 3.2. Legal Frameworks for Space Commerce
The development of the space economy necessitates a clear legal framework that encourages private capital investment without violating the spirit of the 1967 Outer Space Treaty (OST). The critical legal constraint is OST Article II, which prohibits national appropriation of celestial bodies. The US-led Artemis Accords mitigate this risk by clarifying, under Section 10, that resource extraction itself does not inherently constitute national appropriation , thereby establishing a functional legal precedent that de-risks private sector activities authorized by domestic laws like the US Space Resource Exploration and Utilization Act.
In contrast, the 1979 Moon Agreement, which declares space resources the "common heritage of mankind" and demands the establishment of a restrictive international regime before extraction becomes feasible , conflicts directly with the necessary investment model. International law relating to resource extraction often evolves incrementally through state practice and reaction. The strategic approach is to disregard the restrictive, non-binding Moon Agreement while rapidly proliferating the commercial framework established by the Artemis Accords. This strategy of legal incrementalism, prioritizing clarity for capital investment, must be reinforced by a commitment in Phase 3 to negotiate global resource sharing treaties that guarantee equitable benefits to non-signatory and developing nations, honoring global cooperation principles without succumbing to policy paralysis.

## IV. Phase 2: Medium-Term Scaling (2045–2075) – Integration and Preparation
### 4.1. Shifting Economic Motivation: The Pilot of the Contribution Economy
Once UBS has successfully satisfied basic needs, traditional monetary motivation for essential labor diminishes. Phase 2 pilots a shift toward a contribution economy, rewarding voluntary social contribution via non-monetary means, such as social credits for community service.
Governance and Ethical Risks of Digital Reputation Systems
Caution is required when adopting reputation systems, drawing lessons from existing state-run Social Credit System (SCS) pilots, which use centralized scores for rewards and punishments concerning financial and social behavior. If applied within a democracy, such centralized systems risk becoming an "Orwellian system" where the government restricts personal freedoms or punishes dissent based on algorithmic scoring.
To mitigate this severe governance risk, the Contribution Economy must be strictly decentralized and transparent, guided by citizen councils as proposed in participatory economic models. A crucial safety barrier must be enacted: contribution scores must be legally prohibited from linking to or restricting access to basic human rights, essential services, or UBS. Sanctions must be limited to the loss of non-essential social privileges (e.g., priority for grant funding or access to specialized communal resources), guaranteeing that survival remains decoupled from voluntary contribution and preserving fundamental civil liberties.

### 4.2. Scaling Space Infrastructure and ISRU
The medium term is dedicated to building the in-space self-sufficiency necessary for long-term resource return. This is predicated on dramatically reduced launch costs. Current reusable launch systems (Falcon 9) cost approximately $$2,700 per kilogram to Low Earth Orbit (LEO).[span_77](start_span)[span_77](end_span) Starship successors must achieve the projected cost threshold ($200–$$400/kg) and ultimately push costs below $$$100/kg to make orbital construction and bulk transport viable.
ISRU as the Economic Driver
The focus of initial resource extraction shifts to In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU), targeting water and volatiles on the Moon and Near-Earth Objects (NEOs). ISRU is essential because these materials are used in-space for propellant and life support, fundamentally reducing the mass that must be launched from Earth. This ability to produce fuel in space significantly increases the profitability of asteroid mining architectures and lowers the financial break-even point. Mining campaign analyses corroborate this timeline, projecting that the most profitable windows for extraction operations begin between 2047 and 2067.
The analysis establishes a strategic imperative: the space economy must first achieve internal self-sufficiency, a "dual economy" approach where investment prioritizes automated ISRU technology, such as the Project RAMA concepts for converting asteroids into self-propelled spacecraft. This ensures stability and scalability before large-scale bulk resource return to Earth begins in Phase 3.

### 4.3. Measuring True Progress: Adopting the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI)
The full adoption of the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) must be completed in Phase 2. The GPI must replace GDP entirely as the primary metric used in national and international policy reporting. Proponents of GPI argue that it provides a more comprehensive view of a nation's welfare by holistically factoring in social and environmental elements that GDP ignores, such as pollution and volunteerism. The implementation strategy involves leveraging the successful methodologies developed at the US subnational state level to harmonize data collection standards and scale the GPI framework internationally, ensuring a unified global understanding of sustainable economic progress.

## V. Phase 3: Long-Term Realization (2075+) – Space-Enabled Abundance
### 5.1. Large-Scale Asteroid Harvesting and Processing
In the long term, robotic fleets will scale up to harvest resources from large Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) such as 16 Psyche, an asteroid potentially rich in free metals and PGMs (platinum-group metals). The controlled return of these materials, which are scarce on Earth (e.g., platinum, iridium), will flood global markets, effectively eliminating resource scarcity for key technological and industrial inputs. Furthermore, the extraction of Helium-3 from lunar or asteroid regolith will provide the necessary fuel source to power fusion reactors globally, fulfilling the promise of unlimited, clean energy and finalizing the decoupling of energy from terrestrial fossil fuel reliance. Bulk materials mined in space, including silicates and metals, will be utilized to construct massive orbital habitats, such as O’Neill cylinders, providing a mechanism to relieve environmental and population pressures on Earth.

### 5.2. Earth Restoration and the "Earth-First" Policy
Because the blueprint relies on leveraging extraterrestrial resources, it incurs a mandated ethical duty to use the resulting abundance to reverse terrestrial harm. This requires adopting an "Earth-first" policy, where space-sourced materials are explicitly deployed for ecological restoration. For example, mined carbon or space-manufactured materials could be used to facilitate large-scale carbon sequestration infrastructure.
Resource-intensive, engineered ecological restoration techniques—such as aggregate spray seeding on damaged slopes—have demonstrated effectiveness, significantly enhancing carbon storage capacity (up to 189% higher aboveground biomass than unmanaged sites). This establishes that restoration is highly effective when sufficient capital and material resources are applied. This model of reciprocal sustainability requires that Phase 3 treaties explicitly link the influx of space wealth directly to terrestrial restoration outcomes. A defined percentage of PGM and volatile sales must be ringfenced to fund global ecosystem repair, such as investing in AI-monitored biodiversity corridors.

### 5.3. Managing Abundance: Consumption Caps and Planetary Health
The achievement of abundance does not eliminate the risk of environmental harm due to overconsumption, nor does it mitigate the threat of orbital debris (Kessler syndrome) [User Query]. The governance system must impose both philosophical and material restraints, promoting sufficiency lifestyles through public education. Material sustainability will be enforced through AI-optimized distribution systems guided by the adopted GPI metric. These systems will monitor resource flows and implement strategic per-capita consumption caps on certain high-impact or Earth-scarce items, ensuring the long-term health of both the terrestrial environment and the cislunar domain.

## VI. Governance, Geopolitical Risks, and Mitigation Strategies
### 6.1. Strategic Risk Analysis and Inequality Management
The primary domestic risk during the transition is the exacerbation of inequality, should the benefits of automation and space resource utilization be privatized before Universal Basic Services (UBS) are fully deployed. The deployment of the automation tax and aggressive wealth recapture mechanisms must commence immediately in Phase 1 to guarantee equitable access to resources throughout the transition. Globally, the risk of space-enabled neo-colonialism—where wealthy nations monopolize extraterrestrial access—must be managed through mandatory resource sharing treaties in Phase 3. These treaties must be designed to uplift developing regions and prevent resource hoarding, honoring the principle that resources are utilized for the benefit of all states.

### 6.2. Enforcing Planetary Boundaries
Global environmental enforcement requires highly sophisticated, non-territorial governance mechanisms. The enforcement of "planetary boundaries" limits and global carbon budgets relies heavily on advanced AI monitoring and satellite surveillance. AI systems are capable of leveraging extensive data sets, such as laser-captured forest data, to accurately measure Essential Biodiversity Variables (EBVs) and improve carbon accounting. These platforms (e.g., CAPTAIN) can optimize restoration priorities, maximizing both biodiversity conservation and carbon capture simultaneously, while also providing data-driven valuation for biodiversity credits.
Global Governance Frameworks and the Geoengineering Constraint
To enforce harmonized global regulations and prevent a "race to the bottom" driven by economic competition , a Global Post-Scarcity Council (GPC) should be established under the UN framework. This body must possess veto-proof environmental oversight based on intergenerational equity principles.
Critically, the GPC must govern the use of controversial emergency measures like geoengineering (e.g., solar radiation management). While technologically feasible, geoengineering carries immense transboundary risks, including the potential to induce drought in vulnerable regions or intensify hurricanes if deployed unilaterally or without coordination. The political resistance and ethical concerns, including objections from Indigenous communities regarding unauthorized tests , necessitate a stringent governance approach. Geoengineering must be treated solely as a globally governed, high-risk "emergency brake." The GPC’s veto-proof oversight ensures that this capability is controlled exclusively by global scientific consensus, preventing it from becoming a moral hazard that distracts from the crucial Phase 1 and 2 goals of emission reduction and terrestrial resource decoupling.

## VII. Conclusion
The blueprint for achieving a post-scarcity society within a 75-year timeframe is fundamentally an exercise in risk transference and strategic decoupling. Success hinges not merely on technological achievement (fusion, asteroid mining) but on preemptive policy reform in Phase 1 that establishes equity and environmental sustainability as non-negotiable economic mandates.

The structural transition requires the replacement of GDP with the GPI to correctly value environmental health, and the simultaneous implementation of an automation tax to fund Universal Basic Services, thereby transforming technological unemployment into the financial engine of social stability. Legal risks in space must be mitigated by aggressively promoting the commercial clarity of the Artemis Accords while maintaining a future commitment to global equity. By prioritizing energy independence (fusion power) and orbital self-sufficiency (ISRU) in Phase 2, the groundwork is laid for Phase 3, where the injection of vast extraterrestrial resources is utilized not only to guarantee abundance on Earth but also to finance and execute the systematic restoration of the planet. Ultimately, the feasibility of true, long-term post-scarcity is determined by the willingness of contemporary governments to implement restrictive, foundational governance structures—from mandatory circularity to decentralized contribution systems—that ensure abundance serves the collective welfare without triggering environmental collapse or severe transitional inequality.
